What is a good credit score?

Everyone is Googling this factoid “What is a good credit score”. Worry no more! Square Yards has got you covered! To understand what is a good credit score, you should, first of all, know the basics like what is a credit score anyway. 

A credit score is basically a three-digit numerical expression that ranges between 300 to 850 and represents the credit card holder’s creditworthiness. Every lender refers to the credit score before approving the credit application. 

A credit score can be calculated by using the information in your credit report, including the payment history, the amount of debt you have, and also on the length of your credit history. 

In other words, the credit score is used by potential creditors and lenders like credit card companies or banks. It helps them know how capable you are to pay back their money. Although every lender follows a different path for calculating the credit score when it explicitly comes to granting credit. 

Higher credit scores represent that you have a responsible credit behaviour in the past and this, in turn, ensures potential creditors and lenders that you are trustworthy. These days even landlords use the credit score to decide whether they want to keep you as their tenant or not.

How to Improve Your Credit Score?

Let’s have a look at the highly actionable tips and tricks to improve your credit score.

Cancel Unused Credit Cards

Hoarding credit cards won’t do you any good. In fact, it will harm your credit score in the long run. Moreover, there is no need to carry more than two credit cards as more the number of cards, more is the confusion and more accumulation of statements lead to unnecessary mismanagement.

Enhance the credit limit

If your credit card company gives you an offer to increase your credit limit, then accept this offer gladly as it can further help you improve your credit score. Moreover, an increased credit limit comes in handy for emergencies unless you can stop yourself from doing compulsive shopping. 

Analyze your credit card reports recurrently

All the credit card holders once a year should download the credit score report and review it further in order to look for any incorrect listings. If you took any loan from the bank and if you have paid the debt in full and if it is still listed on the credit file, then it will bring down your credit score. If this is not your fault then report the discrepancy to the assigned credit bureau and immediately get them removed from your data to improve credit score. 

Integrate your debts

If you are looking to build your credit score, it is imperative to reduce your debts. We understand it gets intimidating to keep a tab of multiple payments. If you are juggling with the payment of multiple debts then it is best to consolidate all your debts under one umbrella since a single loan offers a lesser rate of interest. You can narrow it down as you will have a single debt to repay and will also help you save tons of money on interest. 

Mitigate your debt-to-Income Ratio

The debt-to-income ratio is a straightforward but indispensable factor to get your credit score back on track. It is basically a debt-to-income ratio of the total debts in regards to your overall income. In a nutshell, a higher debt-to-income ratio signifies a higher level of debt, which further denotes a lower credit score.

Also, if you want to calculate your debt-to-income ratio then you just have to divide your monthly debt payments by your gross monthly income. Moreover, if you want to maintain a good credit score, then try to keep this ratio below 40.

Types of Credit That Impact Your Credit Score

There are basically three types of credit accounts such as installment, open and revolving.

  • Revolving credit is a line of credit that you can borrow easily but has a cap which is well known as the credit limit. A credit limit refers to credit cards and home equity lines of credit. Furthermore, it requires monthly payments and interest charges if you have a balance in your account.
  • An open credit does not come with terms and conditions that you have to pay the loan in a limited time period. Open credit allows the borrower to access credit recurrently up to a specific limit. It is a financial arrangement where once the borrower starts paying back, the money becomes available again for withdrawal as it is a revolving fund.
  • In installment credit, a person borrows a fixed amount and gets ready to make a monthly payment until the full amount of the loan is paid off. For Example: Personal loans, student loans, and mortgages.

How is the Credit Score Calculated?

A credit score is usually calculated by the credit bureaus in the country and each bureau uses its own algorithm. It is basically computed by considering factors such as credit utilisation ratio, payment history, credit type, and credit age. Let’s one by one study these factors in-depth to know the amount of impact they have on your credit score.

Payment History

Payment history has the most significant impact on the credit score. If you always have been persistent and unswerving in paying your loan EMIs and bills, then it outlines that you are a responsible borrower. A responsible credit behaviour suggests that you are at a lower risk of default. Additionally, it makes such types of borrowers eligible for rapid approval on loan applications. Bear in mind, missing payments, making late payments, and many more factors can lower your credit score. 

Credit Utilisation Ratio

Credit Utilisation Ratio is the second most significant factor that can affect credit score. This credit utilisation ratio refers to the total amount of credit you have used in proportion to the total credit limit available to you. You can calculate this ratio by dividing your outstanding balance by your total credit limit. As per experts, consumers should only use 30-40% of the credit limit in mandate to uphold a high credit score. 

Age of the Credit

The age of the credit factor has a medium impact on the credit score. Your history of credit plays a vital role when computing your credit score. If you are a responsible borrower and have always maintained your creditworthiness in the past, then it will positively affect your credit score in the foreseeable future as well. An extended length of credit history helps lenders decide whether they should offer you credit or not. Therefore, it is recommended to keep your credit cards running with a long history rather than applying for a new credit card every next month.

Total Accounts

It is indispensable to maintain a good balance of secured and unsecured credit. If you are maintaining a mixed credit, then it will help you boost your credit score. Though this factor has a lower impact but you cannot ignore this at no cost. This basically reflects the experience you have with handling both secured and unsecured credit types. Just do not keep on borrowing only one type of credit as it can hinder your score. Despite the type of accounts, your credit inquiries are also considered while computing your credit score.

What is a credit payment history?

It encompasses all records regarding your late, on-time, missed payments. In a nutshell, your payment history plays a vital role in determining your overall credit score. What is the difference between credit reports and credit score?

First and foremost, let’s address the elephant in the room: what is a credit report and credit score. Later on, after understanding the meaning we will talk about the difference.

A credit report is a statement that has detailed information about your current credit situation and credit activity like the status of your credit accounts or loan paying history. It has your credit details such as the credit score, length of your credit history, the number of credit accounts owned, the mix of secured and unsecured loans, and also your personal details like employer information, PAN, full name, etc. On the other hand, a credit score is calculated on the basis of the information in your credit report. It is a three-digit number between 300 and 900 and this is an encapsulation of your credit report. If you are fairly good at handling your credit score, then you can expect an excellent credit score.

Both of them, the information on your credit report and your credit score are essential for determining whether you will get a mortgage, auto loan, credit card, or other credit product and what will be the rate of interest that you will be asked to pay. Even your credit score depends on which credit reporting agency provided the information, the type of loan, the scoring model, and the day when it was calculated. If a person has higher scores, it reflects a better loan-paying history and makes it eligible for lower interest rates. However, if there are errors on your credit report, then it can reduce your score- which means an exorbitant rate of interest and less money in your pocket. 

Bottom Line, it is always a pressing priority to check your credit report and rectify any errors before applying for a loan. In a nutshell, credit reports and credit scores are interlinked but aren’t exactly the same.

Does My Credit Score Get Affected If I Enquire About It?

No, your credit score won’t get affected if you enquire about it as checking your own credit is considered a soft inquiry and it does not impact your credit score. Whenever credit score is checked, an inquiry is noted on the credit report. Your inquiry can be classified as either a soft inquiry or hard inquiry depending on who is checking your credit and why it’s being checked. Even though soft inquiries don’t affect your credit scores, hard inquiries can. A hard inquiry takes place when you apply for a loan or credit card and each time the lending institution, bank checks credit score it takes a dip by a few points. Furthermore, if multiple lenders enquire about your credit details within a short span of time, it could impact your credit score.

What is the meaning of soft inquiry?

  • A soft inquiry basically refers to a soft credit check.
  • A soft inquiry takes place when you check your own credit score.
  • When a landlord or employer checks your credit score with your prior permission.
  • When a lender or bank checks your credit score to prequalify you for an offer.

The reason behind soft inquiry not impacting your credit score is that you are not officially applying for credit.

VantageScore, Banks, and FICO create their very own credit scores.
Can they both exist independently?
Absolutely yes, a Credit Report can exist alone as it is a standalone document.
No, a credit score is dependent on a credit report as it is calculated based on the information in a credit report.
How can you procure it?
Every consumer is entitled to receive a free copy of their credit reports once every 12 months from each credit bureau and that too absolutely free of cost.
Lenders are supposed to disclose a credit score and even mortgage lenders are required to show the credit scores. Sometimes FICO and the credit bureaus offer credit scores for free.
How do they help in analysing your creditworthiness?
A credit report encompasses all information regarding consumer’s credit accounts and debts. Also, they include details such as loan amount, history of payment, current balance, and many more.
For calculating a credit score five factors are taken into consideration such as the history of payments, the amount owed, new credit, length of credit history, and mix of credit. Consumers that have scores above 760 are considered to be creditworthy.

What Hurts Your Credit Score?

The question is twisted, it should really be, “What factors can’t hurt your credit score?” We are pretty serious as even a minor mistake on your part can hurt your credit score. Missing a payment, maxing out a card, hard inquiries can hurt your credit score. Now, the real question is what else does? It is definitely a lot to take in so keeping this in mind, Square Yards has compiled a list of 10 things that can hurt your credit score.

Debt Settlement: If you settle a debt with a lender for less than what was originally owed, then it can dampen your credit score from 45 to 125 points.

Closing a credit card account: Cancelling a credit card can decrease your overall credit utilization ratio, leading to a fall in your credit score. Irrespective, it also shortens the overall age of your credit history if you are a credit card holder for a very long time.

Maxing out a credit card: We can’t ignore the fact that credit utilization accounts for 30 percent of the FICO score. The lower your balances are, the better your credit score will be as a maxed-out card can lower your credit score by 10 to 45 points.

Missing a loan payment or credit card payment: Payment history accounts for 35 percent of your FICO score which is a huge figure. As per FICO guidelines, a payment that is 30 days late can cost someone with a low credit score. Nevertheless, card issuers usually do not report late payments to the credit bureaus until they are 60 days late. Keep in mind, a missed payment remark can stay on your credit report for up to seven years.

Hard Inquiries: When you apply for a loan or credit card, then a hard inquiry takes place and at that juncture, the lender reviews your credit report. A hard inquiry stays on the credit report for a maximum of two years, but it only affects the credit score for one year.

Applying for several credit cards: If you are applying for multiple cards in the span of a few months, it will lower your credit score. Additionally, a hard inquiry will add up as this behaviour gives an impression that you are desperate for credit.

Bankruptcy: If someone is declared bankrupt, it can cost him anywhere between 130 to 240 points. Furthermore, a bankruptcy stamp can stay on your credit report for up to 10 years.

Credit report errors: Do you know even trivial credit reporting errors can damage your ability to qualify for loans and credit cards. That is why it is essential to check your credit reports regularly.

Counting on one type of credit: If you are banking on only one type of credit for a long time, then trust us it won’t serve you right in any way. To maximize your credit score rating, use a mix of credit cards.

Debt Consolidation: If someone moves their credit debts into a consolidation loan, then they can expect a slight drop in their credit score because of the hard inquiry. The silver lining is it can help your score to recover back as your credit card balances will be paid off with the loan.

Who Computes Credit Score in India?

The top four credit information bureaus or credit score computing agencies in India are Experian, TransUnion CIBIL, Highmark, and Equifax. 

When anyone makes a transaction, the bank sends details of it to all four credit bureaus that are relevant to determine the credit score. To send details to all credit agencies of your pertinent transactions is mandatory. At any point in time, if banks need to check customer’s online credit score, they can approach any one of the bureaus as they all four are equally authoritative and on par with each other.

Once the credit bureaus receive information from the banks, they get down to the mission of accumulating more information about your creditworthiness and financial habits from other financial institutions. After that, the credit bureaus processes this financial information of the customer to formulate what is called a credit report and credit score.

Why Should I Check My Credit Score Online?

Keeping a close eye on your credit score is a pressing priority. It is one of the best ways to measure your chances of getting a line of credit. Keeping a track of your credit score allows you to make timely amends as it will help you to rectify the errors that are unintentionally made by the credit agencies while calculating your score.

Understanding the Credit Reports Through Key Terms

Square Yards is here to decode the key jargons in a credit report so that it becomes effortless and straightforward to understand it. 

NA or NH

If in your whole life, you never owned a credit card or took a loan from a financial institution, then there are chances that you will see a NA or NH on your credit score. NA or NH signifies that there is no, little, or insufficient credit activity to generate an online report or credit score.

SMA

You will notice SMA on a credit report when you delay the repayments.

STD

It is usually seen on a customer’s credit report where the payments are made with the due dates.

LSS

This remark is seen on a credit card when the lenders report the loan or credit card account as a loss.

DPD

Full form of DPD is days past due which signifies the number of days that the account has not received a single penny of payment.

Written Off/Settled Status: This is shown on the credit report where the borrower fails to make the repayment but makes a settlement with the lender.

DBT

DBT denotes an irresolute situation where the credit information has been disabled for more than 12 months.

Wrapping Up

Once you learn to deal with your credit issues and debt, work on rebuilding your credit score. Start keeping a track of your credit payment history and get ready to manage all your debts. Luckily, a credit score is not set in stone, and you always have a golden chance to improve it. The silver lining over here is you can always boost your credit score. Two cents worth of advice from Square Yards is to use your credit wisely and pay it off consistently without any fail

FAQ

  • Q: Is a credit score of 650 good or bad?
    A: A credit score of 650 is considered good. However, a score below 650 falls in the fair range.
  • Q: Which is the best site to check credit score?
    A: AnnualCreditReport.com is considered to be the best site to check credit score since it allows access to the report from all three prime credit bureaus at no charge- Experian, TransUnion, and Equifax.
  • Q: What is it like to have a very low credit score?
    A: Let say if you have a credit score between 300-549, it indicates poor credibility. As people’s creditworthiness is judged on the basis of credit score, you have to face some solid repercussions like credit and loan applications might not get approved, higher-interest rates on credit cards and loans, pay higher insurance premiums, etc.
  • Q: What is considered a good credit score?
    A: A score between 650-749 is considered good and this score opens the doors for endless benefits such as access to low-interest rates, credit cards, and best of the deals from lenders.
  • Q: What score is too low for a job that does a credit check?
    A: Any credit score that falls below 550 is deemed unacceptable by the employer as the credit score of potential employers is gauged to check the background, trustworthiness, and reliability.
  • Q: What are the best ways to build up my credit score?
    A: One of the best ways to boost your credit score is by building a credit file, not missing any payments, by confining yourself from applying for multiple credit cards, and many more.
  • Q: How to get a personal loan if I have a bad credit score?
    A: Granting a personal loan to a borrower who possesses a bad credit score is a risky proposition. But nothing is impossible. You just have to create your own path and have to win the trust of the banks by proving that your income can support EMI Payments, asking for a lower personal loan amount, involving a guarantor, and rectifying mistakes in your credit report.
  • Q: Is a credit score of 880 just a myth?
    A: Not at all, in fact, even a score of 900, which is the highest possible FICO score, is also achievable. To achieve this range, you just have to work harder as in you have to pay your bills on time, maintain low credit card balances, keep a mix of credits, etc.
  • Q: How to raise my credit score from 500 to 700 in a year?
    A: To raise your credit score from 500 to 700 in a year, you should first of all comprehend what’s hurting your credit and then adopt some highly actionable ways to rebuild it. To rebuild your credit score, do credit monitoring, consolidate your debts, limit your request for new credit, pay your bills on time.
